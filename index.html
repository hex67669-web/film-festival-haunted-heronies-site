ABOUT THE PROGRAMME

Haunted Heroines explores a cycle of Hollywood films produced between 1939 and 1946, a period shaped by war, uncertainty, and a renewed fascination with the past. Drawing on 19th-century Gothic literature, these films reimagine distant settings—moors, mansions, and shadowed interiors—not simply as historical backdrops, but as spaces charged with memory, secrecy, and unease.

Within these environments, what appears to be safe—home, marriage, or intimacy—gradually reveals itself as something more controlling and unstable. From the windswept longing of Wuthering Heights to the oppressive domestic spaces of Rebecca and Jane Eyre, the house becomes a site where identity is shaped and constrained. In Gaslight, this control turns psychological, while The Spiral Staircase pushes it toward fear and vulnerability.

Across these films, the heroine does not simply encounter danger—she enters a system that defines, silences, and contains her.

These women are not only haunted by the past.
They are caught within it.

"Five women. Five houses.
Five ways of being haunted."

Wuthering Heights

PLOT

On the Yorkshire moors, Catherine and Heathcliff’s love endures beyond death, binding landscape, memory, and desire into a single haunting.

WHY THIS FILM

The Gothic begins here—as atmosphere and emotion. Love becomes obsession. The past refuses to remain past.

Rebecca

PLOT

A young bride arrives at Manderley, only to find herself overshadowed by the memory of her husband’s first wife.

WHY THIS FILM

A house can remember. A name can disappear. The heroine is defined by absence—and begins to resist it.

Jane Eyre

PLOT

A governess forms a bond with her employer, while a hidden secret within the house threatens to undo everything.

WHY THIS FILM

Here, the heroine speaks. Moral clarity replaces uncertainty. The Gothic becomes a space of confrontation.

Gaslight

PLOT

A woman begins to doubt her sanity as her husband manipulates her perception of reality.

WHY THIS FILM

The horror is not supernatural. It is psychological. To question reality is to lose control—to reclaim it is to survive.

The Spiral Staircase

PLOT

In a storm-bound mansion, a mute woman is targeted by a killer who preys on vulnerability.

WHY THIS FILM

Silence becomes danger. The gaze becomes violence. The Gothic reaches its most extreme form: to be unseen is to be at risk.

Across these films, the Gothic heroine changes—
from haunted to aware, from passive to resistant.

What remains is the question:

WHO CONTROLS WHAT IS REAL?
