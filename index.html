HAUNTED HEROINES

Gothic Women in 1940s Hollywood

A curated selection of five films exploring fear, memory, and female subjectivity

ABOUT THE SCREENINGS

Haunted Heroines presents a selection of Hollywood films from 1939 to 1946, a period marked by war and instability. During this time, cinema turned inward—away from public life and into enclosed, psychological spaces.

Mansions, corridors, and shadowed interiors are not simply settings, but structures of control. These spaces shape how characters move, see, and remember. What appears safe—home, marriage, intimacy—becomes uncertain, even threatening.

Within these environments, the Gothic heroine emerges. She is watched, silenced, and defined by forces she cannot fully see. Her struggle is not only against danger, but against the conditions that determine her reality.

Across these films, the question is not simply what is real—
but who has the power to define it.

"What appears to be a home is never just a home. It is a structure that shapes what can be seen, what can be known, and what must be believed."

Wuthering Heights

PLOT

On the Yorkshire moors, Catherine and Heathcliff’s love endures beyond death, binding landscape, memory, and desire into a single haunting.

WHY THIS FILM

The Gothic begins here—as atmosphere and emotion. Love becomes obsession. The past refuses to remain past.

Rebecca

PLOT

A young bride arrives at Manderley, only to find herself overshadowed by the memory of her husband’s first wife.

WHY THIS FILM

A house can remember. A name can disappear. The heroine is defined by absence—and begins to resist it.

Jane Eyre

PLOT

A governess forms a bond with her employer, while a hidden secret within the house threatens to undo everything.

WHY THIS FILM

Here, the heroine speaks. Moral clarity replaces uncertainty. The Gothic becomes a space of confrontation.

Gaslight

PLOT

A woman begins to doubt her sanity as her husband manipulates her perception of reality.

WHY THIS FILM

The horror is not supernatural. It is psychological. To question reality is to lose control—to reclaim it is to survive.

The Spiral Staircase

PLOT

In a storm-bound mansion, a mute woman is targeted by a killer who preys on vulnerability.

WHY THIS FILM

Silence becomes danger. The gaze becomes violence. The Gothic reaches its most extreme form: to be unseen is to be at risk.
